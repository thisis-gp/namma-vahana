TRAFFIC DEMAND PREDICTION - HOW OUR SOLUTION WORKS
===================================================

THE TASK
--------
For each road location and each time of day, predict how busy it is (the
"demand", a number between 0 and 1). The score is 100 times R-squared. R-squared
rewards getting the big swings right, so the busy roads matter far more than the
quiet ones.


THE DATA WE WERE GIVEN
----------------------
- Two days of records to learn from. The first day is complete. The second day
  only has the early morning hours.
- A test set we have to predict: the second day, during the daytime.
- For each record we get the location, the time, the road type, the number of
  lanes, whether large vehicles are allowed, nearby landmarks, the weather and
  the temperature.


WHAT WE LEARNED ABOUT THE DATA
------------------------------
- The road type is by far the biggest clue. Highways are busy (around 0.62 on
  average), small streets are in the middle (around 0.27), and residential roads
  are quiet (around 0.06).
- The number of lanes and whether large vehicles are allowed also matter.
- The weather, the temperature and the landmarks turned out to be noise. They
  have almost no effect on how busy a road is, so we do not rely on them.
- Most of the difference between roads comes down to the location itself. A given
  spot tends to be busy or quiet day after day.


THE MODEL
---------
We use XGBoost, a standard and reliable tool for this kind of table of numbers.
It builds a series of small decision trees, each one fixing the mistakes of the
last. We picked it after trying a few alternatives; it gave the best results on
this data.

Three decisions are what pushed the score up.

1. USE BOTH DAYS OF TRAINING DATA.
   Earlier versions only learned from the first, complete day and threw away the
   second morning. Keeping both days lets the model see how the second day
   behaves, and that alone added a little to the score.

2. ADD A "LOCATION AVERAGE" FEATURE.
   For every location we work out its average demand from the training data and
   feed that number in as a clue. This is the single most useful feature, because
   it tells the model up front whether a spot is usually busy or quiet. We work
   it out only from the training data and reuse it for the test set, so there is
   no cheating. We also add how much each location varies.

3. PAY MORE ATTENTION TO BUSY ROADS, AND AVERAGE SEVERAL RUNS.
   Because most roads are quiet, a plain model plays it safe and guesses low
   everywhere. That leaves the busy highways predicted too low, and those are
   exactly the rows the score cares about. So we give each record a weight of
   "1 plus 5 times its demand". A busy highway then gets a weight of about 4 and
   a quiet road about 1.3, so the busy road counts roughly three times as much.
   The model learns to push those busy-road predictions up where they belong.

   How we landed on 5: we tried a range of strengths on a slice of data the model
   had not trained on, and checked which scored best. Weighting by 3, 5 or 8 all
   came out very close. More weight kept helping by a hair, but pushing it too far
   (10 and above) started to hurt the quiet roads. So 5 is a safe middle: strong
   enough to fix the busy-road problem, not so strong that it chases noise or
   ignores everything else.

   We also train the model seven times, each with a different random shuffle, and
   average the seven sets of predictions. Each run is slightly different by
   chance, and averaging smooths out that randomness for a steadier result.


WHAT WE TRIED AND DROPPED
-------------------------
We tested plenty of ideas that did not help, and we left them out rather than
keep clutter:
- Using the exact past value of a location at the exact same time. It sounds
  perfect, but the conditions change from day to day, so it actually made
  predictions worse.
- Deeper, more complex trees. They memorised the training day instead of learning
  the general pattern, and scored lower on the real leaderboard.
- Mixing in other model types. They were weaker here and dragged the result down.
- Dropping the weather and temperature columns. No effect either way, so we left
  the model to ignore them on its own.


THE STEPS IN THE NOTEBOOK
-------------------------
1. Load both days of training data and the test data.
2. Work out each location's average demand and spread.
3. Build the features: time of day, a rush-hour flag, latitude and longitude,
   the location average, lanes-on-a-highway, and simple yes/no columns for the
   text fields.
4. Train XGBoost seven times with the busy-road weighting and average the runs.
5. Save predictions between 0 and 1 to submission.csv.


THE RESULT
----------
This scores about 91.2 on the leaderboard, up from around 90 for the plain
version. The honest limit here is that part of the day-to-day change in traffic
is simply random and cannot be predicted from the columns we were given, so we
focused on getting the steady, predictable part as right as we could.
